# Case Study Objective
A XYZ company is building its analytics capability at the Founders Office level and needs to understand how users move through the platform from registration to active transacting users, which segments and geographies drive the most value, and where product and business interventions can maximise growth and retention.

Business Questions Answered

Q1: How does the user funnel look from registration to activation — and which acquisition channels convert best?

Q2: Which states and business segments drive the most transactions and GTV?

Q3: What is the churn profile — and at what stage do users disengage?

Q4: How have monthly transaction volumes and GTV evolved across FY 2023-24?

Q5: What is the overall transaction quality — and is the success rate a concern?

Q6: How do discounts impact revenue, and which segments receive the most discounts?

# Q1 — User Funnel & Acquisition Channel Analysis
Part A: Registration to Activation Funnel
The user journey on BharatNXT follows a four-stage funnel from registration through KYC completion, account activation, and sustained activity. The analysis reveals strong early-stage conversion but significant attrition at the activation-to-active stage.

Part B: Acquisition Channel Conversion Analysis
Seven referral channels were evaluated on their conversion rate from sign-up to account activation. This reveals which channels attract genuinely high-intent users.
<img width="471" height="140" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-11 at 13 06 03" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f74c6091-c5b7-49c3-867a-6dfabda9dc6c" />
<img width="444" height="217" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/41c26192-be2d-46f7-9294-ebc4c220b7a0" />

KEY INSIGHTS			

• Biggest funnel leak: Registration → KYC (54 users, 10.8%). KYC friction is the #1 early barrier.		

• Post-KYC drop is low (5%): once users complete KYC, most do transact — the product is compelling.				

• Organic Play Store converts best (91.6%) — intentional downloaders have high purchase intent.		

• Referral converts worst (76.8%) — referred users may lack motivation; improve referral onboarding.		

• 49% of activated users have churned — retention, not acquisition, is the bigger problem.							

Recommendations

•	Double down on Play Store ASO and Partner Channel — both deliver high volume and high conversion.

•	Redesign the referral incentive structure: tie bonuses to first transaction completion, not just sign-up.

•	Run a targeted re-engagement campaign for the 54 incomplete KYC users — in-app nudges with simplified document submission.

•	A/B test the App Store listing and onboarding flow to close the gap with Play Store conversion.

• Telangana tops GTV (₹7.77 Cr) despite fewer active users — suggesting high-value SMEs in Hyderabad.		

• Karnataka shows high registrations (60) but weak activation (41.67%) — a priority market for activation campaigns.			

• Delhi has the highest activation rate (50%) & High registrations (60) but relatively low GTV — volume market, not high-ticket.

•	Run activation-focused campaigns in Karnataka and Punjab to recover the registered but inactive cohort.

•	Treat Delhi and Tamil Nadu as growth markets for volume — lower convenience fee thresholds may unlock more SME use cases.

•	Prioritise West Bengal for a pilot partner channel program — organic reach is clearly insufficient.

# Q3 — Churn Analysis (60-Day Inactivity Rule)
Part A: Overall Churn Metrics
Churn is defined as a user whose last transaction date was more than 60 days before the analysis date (March 2024). This represents a conservative, activity-based definition that does not rely on platform-assigned status alone.

Churn by Acquisition Channel

Key Insights

•	The Referral channel, despite being the lowest converter, also has the highest churn rate (58.1%) — this channel is bringing in low-commitment users who don't stick.

•	Partner Channel not only converts well (86.7%) but also has the lowest churn rate (45.8%) — the channel that brings quality users who stay.

•	Even the best channel (Play Store) churns 48.7% of users — the churn problem is systemic across all channels, not channel-specific.

•	Digital Ad has a relatively low churn rate (46.9%) considering it's a paid channel — suggesting paid users have sufficiently high intent.

Part B: Shallow Churn — Early Dropout Analysis
Shallow churn identifies users who placed only 1-2 txns before going dormant, they tried the platform but failed to find enough value to continue.
<img width="561" height="152" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-11 at 13 48 47" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e971e29b-44df-4ba5-813f-62d70805ba92" />
<img width="388" height="217" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5ed64eec-6a9f-49ab-b8e8-2500f26858e2" />

Key Insights

•	66.3% of churned users had 21+ transactions before going silent — these are NOT shallow churners, they were genuinely engaged users.

•	Only 2.4% of churned users had 1-2 transactions — shallow churn is NOT the primary problem. The platform is losing its best users.

•	The 21+ txn churned cohort likely contains high-GTV users who found an alternative or experienced a service failure — this is the highest-value re-engagement target.

•	Users in the 11–20 bucket (18.8%) represent a 'tipping point' cohort — they were close to becoming habitual users but something broke the habit.

Recommendations

•	Launch a 'win-back' campaign specifically for 21+ transaction churned users, these are proven high-value users who left for a reason.

•	Implement a proactive outreach trigger at 45 days of inactivity — before the 60-day churn threshold is reached.

•	Investigate WHY 21+ transaction users churned: was it fee pricing? A gateway failure? A competitor offer? Conduct exit interviews.

•	Restructure referral incentives: pay the bonus only after the referred user completes 5+ transactions to reduce low-quality referrals.

•	Build a 'streak' or loyalty mechanic for users approaching 20 transactions to push them into the 21+ habitual segment.

Monthly trends across FY 2023-24 reveal a platform still in the growth phase, with strong Q2 performance and a notable activation surge in Q4 (Jan-Mar 2024).

KEY INSIGHTS		

• Strong growth trajectory: GTV grew from ₹39 Mn in Apr-23 to ₹2,587 Mn in Mar-24 — a 66× increase.		

• March-24 spike: Txn count (4,939) and GTV are ~65% above the prior month — likely year-end GST / vendor payment rush.			

• Seasonal dip in Aug-Sep: lower activity post-monsoon before Q3 pickup — aligns with typical SME payment cycles.		

• Instant (Same Day) has been gaining share from ~50% in H1 to ~55% in H2 — users prefer speed despite higher fees.		

Recommendations

•	Build a financial year-end campaign (Feb–Mar) targeting tax payments, GST filing, and vendor settlement — the March spike is repeatable and should be planned for.

•	Address the Aug–Sep dip proactively with targeted promotions or lower fee periods to maintain momentum.

•	Monitor the Classic vs Instant GTV split monthly — if Instant share grows, consider whether the fee differential is sustainable.

•	Use the Jan-Mar cohort performance to model LTV for all future acquisition campaigns.
# Q5 — Transaction Quality & Success Rate Analysis
Success Rate by Payment Mode
Transaction quality was evaluated across both payment modes — Classic (T+3) and Instant (Same Day) — to determine whether mode is a driver of success or failure.
<img width="660" height="78" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-11 at 14 02 32" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/88ba4532-115b-4b43-aa13-8a532dd0323d" />
<img width="595" height="215" alt="Screenshot 2026-04-11 at 14 02 47" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/d4d90f3d-86c8-468b-b0af-645430b632ec" />
<img width="447" height="224" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/ebf05304-1fee-48ec-a9a4-517fca3ac000" />

KEY INSIGHTS			

• Overall success rate: 66.5% — consistent across both Classic (66.6%) and Instant (66.3%) payment modes.		

• Mode is NOT a driver of failure — success rates are nearly identical for both modes.		

• Apr-23 had the lowest success rate (61.2%) — likely early-stage infrastructure issues or tiny sample (only 85 txns).		

• Success rates are remarkably stable at 64–69% all year — systemic issue, not event-driven. Gateway reliability?		

• ~17% of txns remain Pending — some will eventually settle; worth tracking settlement conversion rate.				

Recommendations

•	Investigate root causes of the 16.7% failure rate urgently — if this is a bank/gateway issue, negotiate SLA improvements or add alternative gateway redundancy.

•	Build a Pending transaction monitoring dashboard: track how many Pending transactions convert to Success within 24-48 hours vs. remain stuck.

•	Consider implementing a retry mechanism for failed transactions with user notification to recover GTV.

•	Set a target success rate of 75%+ as a 6-month OKR — each percentage point improvement on 26,936 transactions is meaningful revenue.

•	Tag failed transactions by failure reason (bank decline, insufficient limit, gateway timeout) to prioritise the fix roadmap.

KEY INSIGHTS

• ~ 32% of transactions are discounted.

• Revenue foregone is ₹10 Cr (~ 32% of potential revenue) — significant amount.

• Avg transaction amount is marginally lower for discounted txns (₹5.20L vs ₹5.36L) — discounts are NOT reserved for big tickets.		

• Wholesale Pharmasegments receive the most discounts (10.92%) — consistent with volume-based pricing strategy for B2B bulk payers.			

• Discounting is broadly consistent across channels (9.2%–10.9% range) — no obvious channel-favouritism.		

• Hypothesis: Discounts are applied as retention incentives to high-frequency or at-risk users, not systematically by segment.		

• Recommendation: Tie discounts to transaction volume thresholds (e.g., ₹50L+ GTV/month) for a transparent tiered pricing model.
